Research context

This research investigates the administrative, legal, and emotional labour that whānau and executors perform when someone dies in Aotearoa — work that is real, extensive, and largely invisible to the system that depends on it.

How does Aotearoa's death care system shape the agency of whānau navigating it, and under what conditions might emerging technologies redistribute that agency?

The artefacts on this page make that invisible work visible. They are based on cross-transcript synthesis of interviews with whānau who have navigated the death arc, and analysis of 36 NZ statutes and regulations governing the process. All task positions and complexity judgements are qualitative and are offered for validation, not as settled findings.

None of the material on this page constitutes legal advice.

Feedback from practitioners and whānau

If you are viewing this as a practitioner, sector participant, or whānau member with experience navigating the death arc, your response to these artefacts matters. The research is trying to understand:

  • Does the swimlane journey map reflect the experience of families you have supported or your own experience?
  • Are there tasks missing, mislabelled, or placed in the wrong phase or lane?
  • Does the document-gated dependency map accurately reflect how the system sequences?
  • Are there places where the system works better than these artefacts suggest — moments of agency, support, or grace that should be shown?
  • Does the urgency–complexity matrix place tasks in positions that feel right, or are there surprises?
  • What is missing from this picture entirely?
